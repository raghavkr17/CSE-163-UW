# Food Desert Analysis

## Is the plot produced by plot_food_access_by_county an effective visualization? Why or why not?
Mostly yes. It makes it easy to compare counties and it keeps the same scale for all four maps, so you can actually line things up in your head. The weakness is that it is still a color gradient map, so small differences can be hard to notice, and it is not great for people with color vision issues. Also, county level averages can hide a lot of variation inside one county.


## Is the plot produced by plot_low_access_tracts an effective visualization? Why or why not?
It is effective for spotting where the low access tracts are, because the highlight stands out on top of the base map. But it is also kind of blunt. It turns a tract into yes or no, and you do not see how close a tract was to the cutoff. You also do not see how many people are affected inside each highlighted tract.

## What is one way that government officials could use plot_food_access_by_county or plot_low_access_tracts to shape public policy on how to improve food access?
They could use the county ratios to decide where to prioritize funding, like grocery store incentive programs, mobile markets, or transit improvements to food stores. The tract map is also useful for picking specific areas to focus on, since it shows where the low access tracts cluster.

## What is a limitation or concern with using the plots in this way? What information might be missing from these plots?
The plots do not include things like transit, car ownership, food prices, store quality, or how safe it is to get to a store. They also do not tell you why a place is low access, only that it meets the threshold. On top of that, the data is from 2010, so using it alone could push decisions based on conditions that have changed.
